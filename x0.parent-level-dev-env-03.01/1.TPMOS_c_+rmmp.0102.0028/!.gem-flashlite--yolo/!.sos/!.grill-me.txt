To scan a project for Ubiquitous Language and Domain-Driven Design (DDD) effectively, your prompt should treat the AI as a "Domain Architect" tasked with identifying inconsistencies between business intent and technical implementation. [1, 2] 
## The "Domain Architect" Scan Prompt

Role: You are an expert Domain-Driven Design (DDD) Architect.
Task: Scan the provided [Codebase/Documentation/Spec] to identify the project's Ubiquitous Language and evaluate its DDD Maturity.
Analysis Requirements:

   1. Extract the Glossary: Identify recurring domain-relevant nouns (Entities/Value Objects) and verbs (Domain Events/Commands). Map them to their apparent Bounded Contexts.
   2. Detect Language Friction:
   * Ambiguity: Where is the same word (e.g., "Order") used for different concepts in different contexts?
      * Synonyms: Where are different words (e.g., "User" vs. "Client") used for the same concept?
      * Technical Leaks: Flag technical jargon (e.g., "ProcessData", "UserRow") that should be replaced with business-centric terms.
   3. Pattern Audit: Identify potential DDD tactical patterns:
   * Aggregates: Which entities act as consistency boundaries?
      * Value Objects: Are primitive types (strings/ints) being used for domain concepts that have rules?
   4. Output:
   * A canonical Glossary (term, definition, context).
      * A list of "Language Smells" with specific refactoring suggestions.
   
Input Context: [Paste your code snippets or project description here]

## Refinement Checklist for Your "Grill-Me" Session
To reach a "shared understanding" as you requested, let's start the walk down the design tree. I need to grill you on these three foundational dependencies first:

   1. The "Core" Problem: DDD is for complex domains. Is this project a "CRUD" application (simple data entry) or a complex system with deep business logic? If it’s simple, full DDD might be over-engineering.
   2. The Domain Expert: Who is the source of truth for the language? If there isn't a non-technical expert involved, the "Ubiquitous Language" often just becomes "Developer Slang" in disguise.
   3. The Bounded Contexts: Have you already identified where the meaning of terms changes (e.g., a "Product" in the Catalog vs. a "Product" in Shipping)? This is the most common point of failure. [8] 

Which of these three areas feels the shakiest in your current plan?
