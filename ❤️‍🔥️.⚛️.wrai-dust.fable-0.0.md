## 3. 🪄 The Fusion: How the Three Pillars Become One Sim

```
        ⚛️ DUSTOPIA (physics/chem law)
              │  size=time, fuzz pets, fractal zoom
              ▼
        🐾 XO-PETS (creatures + world mechanics)
              │  entities live inside Dustopia's rules
              ▼
        💹 MAR$.$TREETRACE (society on top of creatures)
              │  pets/players form corporations, governments,
              │  economies, trade, news cycles
              ▼
        🪟 WRAITH (the shell that renders + hosts all of it)
```

- **Dustopia supplies the "why does this material act this way" layer.** Instead of `xo-pets` needing a separate "crafting rules" system and `Mar$.$treetRace` needing a separate "resource value" system, both can read off the *same* Dustopia scale-and-bond rules: a material's rarity, reactivity, and value all fall out of where it sits on the fractal/spectral scale. One rulebook, two consumers. ✅ (This is exactly the shared-rule discipline [`vision-roadmap-index.md`](../%23.plans-tpmt/vision-roadmap-index.md) already asks for: *"If a feature naturally crosses multiple projects, it probably needs a shared contract."*)
- **`xo-pets` supplies the "who/what is moving around" layer.** Fuzz Pets aren't just lore — they can literally BE the pet/entity layer `xo-pets` already knows how to spawn, select, and animate. A Dustopia particle and an `xo-pets` creature are the same data shape at different `λ` (zoom).
- **`Mar$.$treetRace` supplies the "what do they build together" layer.** Once pets/players exist in a Dustopia-ruled world, `Mar$.$treetRace`'s corporations/governments/multiverse machinery gives them something to *do*: found companies that mine/refine Dustopia materials, governments that tax and regulate discoveries, a news ticker that reports on it, multiple parallel worlds (`multiverse/`) for different rulesets or servers.
- **Wraith supplies the eyes.** Per [`vision-roadmap-wraith.md`](../%23.plans-tpmt/vision-roadmap-wraith.md), Wraith is "the visual shell and host runtime," not where game logic should live. So Dustopia+xo-pets+Mar$.$treetRace stay as file-backed state and ops; Wraith just renders whichever piece is focused. That boundary is important — **don't let Wraith become a fourth copy of the rules.** 🚫🔁

## 11. 🧫 Deep Dive: From 0/1/Z/3 to Molecules to Simple Cells to Drug Screening (expands §5)

The transistor-logic analogy is literal, not just a vibe: in digital electronics a wire is `0` (low), `1` (high), `Z` (floating/high-impedance, not driven either way), or in a bus-conflict/overload case an undefined "cutoff" state. Dustopia reuses exactly that 4-symbol vocabulary as a **bond state between two materials** instead of a wire's voltage:

- `0` — **no bond.** Two materials sit next to each other, nothing happens (sand next to sand).
- `1` — **bonded/driven.** A stable bond forms — this is what turns two loose materials into a molecule, or a molecule into part of a bigger structure.
- `Z` — **pass-through / high-impedance.** Neither side forces state onto the other — this is what gives a material "channel" or "membrane" behavior: things can move past it without reacting with it.
- `3` — **cutoff/breakdown.** An overload/incompatible marker — an existing bond is forcibly broken, or a structure decomposes. This is where "toxic" or "unstable" outcomes live.

**Worked Example 1 — a bonding table, not an equation.** When material A meets material B, look up `bond(A,B)` in a small table:
```
0 + 0 -> 0   (no reaction)
0 + 1 -> 1   (B drives A into a bond — weak, one-directional)
1 + 1 -> 1   (mutual bond — stable molecule forms)
Z + x -> x   (Z is transparent — x passes through unchanged)
3 + x -> 0   (3 forcibly breaks any existing bond back down to 0)
```
Same shape as a transistor truth table: a handful of rows, O(1) lookup, no orbitals, no differential equations. This is not simulating real chemistry — it's simulating the *idea* of bonding cheaply enough to run across thousands of voxels at 60fps.

**Worked Example 2 — a membrane, and then a "cell," from bonds alone.** Tile a hollow shell of `1`-bonded material around an empty interior. Give the shell's outward face a `Z` (pass-through) property and its inward face a `1` (bonded) property — that asymmetry alone is a working definition of a membrane: some things pass, some things don't. Cycle a small amount of `Z`-state material in and out of that shell on a timer — a toy stand-in for **metabolism**. Add one rule: "once interior `1`-bonded material crosses threshold N, spawn a duplicate shell nearby with the same rules" — a toy stand-in for **replication**. Boundary + throughput loop + replication trigger, all from the same four symbols already defined above. This is "simple cell" in the same sense Conway's Game of Life is "simple life": a minimal rule-set producing cell-*like behavior*, not a biologically accurate cell.

**Worked Example 3 — drug testing on that toy cell.** A "drug" is just another material with its own bond-state profile, introduced next to the toy cell. Run the same bonding table between the drug and the cell's membrane/interior materials, and classify the result automatically:
- Blocked at the membrane (`Z`, no bond forms) → **"no effect, doesn't reach target."**
- Bonds (`1`) with the membrane and breaks its outward-`Z`/inward-`1` asymmetry → **"toxic, disrupts structure."**
- Bonds (`1`) with an interior material that had drifted to `0` and restores its cycling → **"therapeutic candidate, restores function."**
- Triggers `3` anywhere in the structure → **"causes breakdown."**

Batch it: generate a few hundred candidate materials by varying one or two parameters (their `λ`/scale, which bond state they present), run all of them against the same toy cell, rank the logged outcomes. That's a real, recognizable workflow shape — **virtual/high-throughput screening** — running on a toy rule-table instead of real molecular dynamics. The honest pitch stays the same as §5: this is a fast way to *prototype the shape of a screening experiment*, generate hypotheses, or teach the concept — not a substitute for real cheminformatics (docking, ADMET, wet-lab work). Anyone doing actual pharmacology needs the real tools; this sandbox just makes the *idea* of the workflow tangible and fun before/alongside that.

## 12. 🧠 The FSM Brain: One Chassis, Four Ways to Choose (expands §4)

This one doesn't need inventing — it's already specified in the repo. `#.fsm+ai-training-plan-b1/fsm_bot_programmer.txt` already defines a shared **FSM Bot Programmer** used identically for testing bots and AI bot players, PAL-scriptable via op-ed; `#.fsm+ai-training-plan-b1/ai_plan.txt` already defines an **ai-labs** project for distillation, training, and per-instance "personality" pieces. Dustopia Sim's whole AI layer (§4) is just: **every AI-driven thing in the sim — pet, corporation board, government, drug-candidate researcher — is one of these FSM pieces**, and each one plugs in a different "how do I choose" strategy at its decision points.

**The chassis (already-specified shape):**
```
projects/<sim>/pieces/<agent>/
├── piece.pdl
├── state.txt            # current_state=idle, decision_mode=preset|weighted|rl|llm
├── fsm/states.txt        # idle, deciding, acting, ...
├── fsm/transitions.txt   # idle -> deciding (always), deciding -> acting (chosen), ...
└── events/on_tick.asm     # PAL script: dispatch on current_state, call bot::* ops
```
Every agent is the same piece shape. What differs is only what happens at the "deciding" state — controlled by one field, `decision_mode`.

- **Tier 1 — Preset scripts (`decision_mode=preset`).** The PAL script hard-codes the choice, exactly like `fsm_bot_programmer.txt`'s existing AI-bot-player example (`bot::choose r5, 3` → uniformly random among attack/defend/flee). Zero cost, deterministic if seeded — where most of the sim's background pets/props should live.
- **Tier 2 — Weighted sets (`decision_mode=weighted`).** Same FSM, same `bot::choose`-style dispatch, but options are picked from a small `weights.txt` (`attack=0.5, defend=0.3, flee=0.2`) instead of uniformly. Still no model call, still cheap, but now tunable without touching PAL code — and it's exactly what `ai_plan.txt`'s `train <curriculum>` step can update over time: run a batch, log which choice correlated with a better `bot::record_pass`/`bot::record_fail`-style outcome, nudge that option's weight up. A real, if simple, form of reinforcement learning — no LLM required.
- **Tier 3 — RL proper (`decision_mode=rl`).** Same interface, but the weight lookup is replaced by a small learned policy keyed on `(state.txt snapshot, available options) → chosen option`, trained offline from the `(state, action, outcome)` tuples the FSM already produces for free — every tick already writes `state.txt`, every bot op already reports a pass/fail-shaped result. No bespoke telemetry needed; the training step just reads the piece history files that already exist.
- **Tier 4 — LLM curriculum (`decision_mode=llm`).** For the handful of agents where judgment/novelty matters — corporation strategy, government policy, an R&D agent proposing a genuinely new drug candidate instead of replaying known ones — the "deciding" state hands the local model (`gem-dev`/`cpp-llm` lane) exactly two things: the agent's `state.txt`, and its personality piece (already scaffolded per `ai_plan.txt`'s Chat Instance Manager). Critically, **the model's only output is one of the FSM's already-enumerated option names** — it never gets direct write access to state; PAL dispatches the chosen op exactly like every other tier. That bounded-vocabulary constraint keeps an LLM-driven agent just as file-auditable, and just as swappable back to `preset` for debugging, as everything else.

**Why one chassis for all four matters:** `fsm_bot_programmer.txt` already insists testing bots need **deterministic, seeded** behavior for regression tests. Because `decision_mode` is just a field on an otherwise-identical piece, any LLM- or RL-driven agent can be flipped back to `preset` with a fixed seed and replay the *exact* scenario it was in — states, transitions, and the action vocabulary never change, only who's doing the choosing. The sim never accumulates four incompatible AI systems; it accumulates one FSM with four interchangeable brains — the same reuse discipline the rest of this fable, and `vision-roadmap-index.md`'s shared-rule principle, already commit to.

**Tie-back to §11:** the drug-screening agent from the worked example above is just another FSM piece — `idle -> propose_candidate -> synthesize -> test_on_cell -> record_result -> idle` — and `propose_candidate`'s `decision_mode` is the one knob deciding whether it tries a fixed list (`preset`), biases toward what's worked before (`weighted`/`rl`), or asks a local model to suggest something genuinely new given the cell's history (`llm`). Same chassis, different brain, same audit trail.

## 13. 🌱 Long-Horizon: From Cell Evolution to General Skills (Chat / Cook / Teach / School / Drive)

**Flagging this up front: this is the farthest-out, most speculative layer in the whole doc — a direction to grow toward, not a near-term deliverable.** It belongs in §15's build order dead last, after everything else in this doc is solid.

The claim: the same toy-cell substrate from §11 (minerals bonding via `0/1/Z/3` into membranes, metabolism loops, replication) is also the training ground for FSM agents (§12) to acquire general-purpose skills, not just drug-candidate screening. The mechanism isn't magic, it's the existing Tier 3/4 loop run for longer and on more tasks:

- Cells/agents that cooperate (two toy cells whose `Z`-channels line up, letting resources trade instead of just cycling internally) survive/replicate more in a simulated environment where resources are scarce — that's a selection pressure, cheaply computed from the same bond table.
- Whatever behavior pattern correlates with surviving/cooperating gets logged as `(state, action, outcome)` tuples exactly like §12's Tier 3, then distilled (`ai_plan.txt`'s `distill <vocab_piece> <source_text>` step, generalized from text corpora to behavior corpora) into a small curriculum model.
- "Chat," "cook," "teach," "go to school," "drive" are just **names for FSM job-roles** — each one is a `piece.pdl` with its own `fsm/states.txt`/`transitions.txt` (e.g. a "cook" bot: `idle -> select_recipe -> gather_ingredients -> combine(bond table) -> serve -> idle`) sitting on the exact same chassis as §12, trained the exact same tiered way. There is no separate "general intelligence" module to build — general-seeming behavior is what you get from running the same small chassis across many job-shaped curricula and letting weighted-sets/RL/LLM tiers compound over time.
- **The honest caveat, stated as plainly as §5/§11's:** getting from "toy cells cooperating over minerals" to "an agent that can actually cook, teach, or drive well" is an enormous, multi-year distillation and curriculum-design effort, not a feature to schedule. What's realistic *today* is the chassis and the tiering (§12) — this section is here so the chassis gets built in a way that doesn't have to be re-architected later if/when ambition grows toward these job-roles.

## 14. 🔬📉 Fractal Zoom Isn't Just For Pixels — It Applies to AI Compute and Data Too

Dustopia's core physics trick (§2/§3: compress to coarse cached properties when zoomed out, expand to detail when zoomed in) turns out to already have a sibling in this codebase's own conventions, and §12's four-tier `decision_mode` is secretly the same trick applied to AI cost instead of pixels:

- **Emoji Studio already does exactly this for voxel detail:** `projects/wraith-alpha/wraith-projects/emoji-studio-wraith/` stores each emoji as `voxels_8.csv`, `voxels_16.csv`, `voxels_32.csv`, `voxels_64.csv` — the *same* asset at four zoom resolutions, picked per-use (`IMAGE_EXTRUSION | resolution=8 | ...` in its receipt format). `projects/fuzz-op-gl/assets/voxels/xelector.voxel` is independently 8×8×8. **"Make all voxels 8x8x8"** should mean: standardize the *default/base* resolution every voxel-emitting system falls back to on the `8` tier (matching both existing anchors above), and treat `16/32/64` as opt-in zoom-in detail layers rather than a second competing convention — the same "coarse by default, detailed on demand" rule Dustopia already uses for materials, just applied to the emoji/tile asset pipeline instead of physics.
- **§12's `decision_mode` tiers are the same zoom applied to AI cost:** `preset` is the "fully zoomed out" cached/coarse answer (cheapest, used for the vast majority of background agents, exactly like a far-away tile only needing its coarse cached density/color); `weighted`/`rl` is a medium zoom (a small lookup/policy, cheap but tunable); `llm` is "fully zoomed in" (expensive, used only for the one agent currently being paid attention to — a corporation board mid-decision, a drug-candidate researcher on a genuinely novel case). This is why the tiering in §12 also happens to be the answer to "save space/compute": **most agents, most of the time, should be running at the cheapest tier**, with detail (and cost) only paid for whichever handful of agents are currently in-focus — the AI equivalent of only rendering the tiles inside the camera frustum.

## 17. 🎛️ Modular Control Surfaces — and the Real Goal

Controls for companies and for pets don't need their own bespoke UI system each: they can share the same external menus Wraith/op-ed already render, **or** a user can build a fully custom menu for how *they specifically* want to drive their pets, their FSM, or their corporation-of-pets. None of this needs new architecture: it's §12's `decision_mode` modularity applied to the *control surface* instead of the *decision strategy* — whichever menu/UI is driving a piece is just another swappable front-end over the same piece.

**That swap-ability is the actual point of this whole document, stated plainly:** the goal is **modularity of editable AI systems, in exactly the same sense games are already editable** (op-ed's levels, PAL's scripts, Wraith's layouts). And the same way §12/§13 describe distilling behavior into small curriculum-tuned models, the plan for building all of this is recursive too: train the AI itself to research and build new features using PAL scripts, and treat a large frontier model (the same kind of model writing this document) as a *source* to diffuse into small, manually-tuned curriculum corpuses (`ai_plan.txt`'s Knowledge Distillation Lab, already scaffolded) — not as a permanent dependency. Big AI helps bootstrap; the destination is small, local, editable, and yours.
